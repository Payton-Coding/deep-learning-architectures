# deep-learning-architectures Reflection

Which architecture was most effective for its task?
After comparing the architectures from this module, I believe the Convolutional Neural network (CNN) was the most effective match for its assigned task. The Multilayer Perceptron (MLP) performed well on MNIST because handwritten digits are fairly simple, but it had to flatten every image into a long row of pixel values. The CNN kept the two-dimensional image structure and learned small visual features such as edges, curves, and textures. That made it more appropriate for Fashion-MNIST, where categories such as shirts, coats, and pullovers can look very similar. The confusion matrix and incorrect-image examples were especially helpful because they showed that accuracy alone does not explain everything. The model could classify obvious items such as bags and ankle boots more easily, while visually similar clothing classes were harder to separate. The LSTM was also a strong fit for IMDB reviews because it processed words as a sequence and used earlier context when deciding whether a review was positive or negative. Overall, each architecture worked best when its design matched the structure of the data.

What challenges did you face during training or implementation?
One of the biggest challenges I ran into was understanding that each neural network architecture expects the data to be prepared in a different way. Even though all of the models were built using TensorFlow, the inputs were not the same. The MLP was the easiest to work with because it only needed the MNIST images flattened into a single row of pixel values. The CNN was a little more challenging because I had to reshape the images by adding a channel dimension so TensorFlow would recognize them as grayscale images. For the IMDB dataset, the reviews were already converted into numbers, but since every review had a different number of words, I had to pad them, so they were all the same length before training the LSTM.
The DCGAN was definitely the most difficult part of the assignment. Instead of training one model, I had to train both a generator and a discriminator at the same time. It took me a while to understand how those two models work together, since one is trying to create realistic images while the other is trying to tell whether the images are real or fake. Another challenge was that the training results weren't as straightforward as the other models because there wasn't a simple accuracy score to follow. 

How do you see these models being used in real-world applications?
These architectures have clear real-world uses because they solve different types of problems. An MLP could analyze structured business data for tasks such as income prediction, fraud detection, or customer-risk scoring. CNNs are useful where visual information matters, including medical imaging, airport security screening, damaged-equipment detection, and automated inspection. An RNN or LSTM could examine sequences such as customer reviews, help-desk conversations, weather measurements, flight activity, or passenger-volume trends. The IMDB example showed how a sequence model can recognize overall sentiment even when reviews contain many words. A DCGAN can generate synthetic images that may support creative work or increase the amount of training data available when real examples are limited. However, generated data would need to be reviewed carefully because unrealistic or biased examples could reduce model quality. This assignment showed me that choosing a deep learning model should begin with the type of data and the goal of the project rather than simply selecting the most complicated architecture.
